Computational Geospatial Science

I develop open-source geospatial techno-economic models for assessing the viability of different Internet broadband connectivity options. I apply spatial optimization models including Steiner Trees to design and plan broadband networks to estimate maximum capacity coverage and minimize costs and carbon emissions.

Brief Project Description

Here some of the past and current projects on assesssing broadband connectivity options as well as the role of Internet in improving climate change resilience.

  • Mobile Broadband Geospatial Model

    With over 2.6 billion still unconnected to the Internet, new mobile technology systems including 5G and 6G will be crucial in narrowing digital divide, thanks to higher penetration of smartphones. This model evaluates and compares the capacity, coverage, cost and environmental impact of connecting the unconnected using 4G and 5G.

  • Broadband Connectivity and Climate Change Resilience

    While there has been a greater focus on quantifying the relationship between poverty and natural hazard vulnerability, no existing analysis quantifies these effects when also considering cellphone coverage (given cellphones provide a strong mitigation strategy via early warning messages). This is further compounded by the lack of open source geospatial data on the number of unconnected people at a global scale. One proxy for quantifying the number of unconnected users, is information on those regions uncovered by existing mobile cellular technologies, such as 2G, 3G or 4G.

  • Fixed Fiber Broadband

    Fixed fiber provides an option for connecting some of the unconnected areas through innovative network layout such as Fiber-to-the Neighborhood (FTTnb) architecture. Unlike satellite, fixed fiber is considered as one of the greenest forms of broadband capable of providing descent speeds. Here an open source integrated geospatial model is developed for estimating the capacity, coverage, cost and emissions of fixed fiber broadband systems in connecting the population below the poverty line and uncovered population in rural areas.

Space in Africa

Reports of vandalism of metal parts at the defunct Longonot Earth Station was a low point for the Kenyan space community. ICT Cabinet Secretary Joe Mucheru’s response on Twitter was even more disheartening. His response was that the facility has been non-operational for decades and he does not manage private companies since it is now in the hands of Telkom Kenya. While it is true that the facility is outdated, there are proposals to convert it to a radio astronomy research facility. Such conversion has been done in Ghana in collaboration with the South African government. In a bid to prove to the world’s largest science instrument project, Square Kilometre Array (SKA), South Africa promised to build pre-cursor telescopes in the country and the rest of Africa to help investigate secrets of the universe. Are we alone in the universe? Was Einstein right? What happened before the formation of stars and planets? These are some of the questions that South Africa and the SKA project seek to answer. In the spirit of Ubuntu, as they say in South Africa, the project extended collaboration to other eight African countries. These are Kenya, Botswana, Namibia, Ghana, Mozambique, Madagascar, Zambia and Mauritius..
